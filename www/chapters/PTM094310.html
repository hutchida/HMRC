Valuing Pension Savings For Individual Protection 2016

0

1

Paragraphs 9(1)(a), (1)(b) and (4) and 10 to 13 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

2

To apply for individual protection 2016 (IP 2016) a member must calculate the value of their pension savings as they stood on 5 April 2016. The way in which pension savings are valued depends on what they consist of. The valuation of an individual’s pension savings is the total of four amounts (amounts A to D) as follows:

3

Amount A - any pension that the member started to receive before 6 April 2006 ,

4

Amount B - any pension that came into payment after 5 April 2006 but before 6 April 2016 (along with certain tax-free lump sums received in the same period),

5

Amount C - pension savings that the member has not yet taken from their registered pension scheme, and

6

Amount D - pension savings that the member has not yet taken from certain overseas pension schemes.

7

How to obtain a valuation

8

How to obtain a valuation

9

Regulation 14C The Registered Pension Schemes (Provision of Information) Regulations 2006 – SI 2006/567

10

In order to apply for IP 2016 a valuation will need to be obtained of the value of the member’s pension savings at 5 April 2016. However, HMRC will accept asset valuations already obtained to be treated as the value of the asset on 5 April 2016 where that valuation is at a date from 31 March 2016 to 4 April 2016 and there has been no material change in the assets between that date and 5 April 2016.

11

A member can ask their pension scheme provider to give them a value for their pension savings on 5 April 2016 or tell them (if they are not already aware of it) what their annual rate of pension was on that date. A scheme administrator must give this information to the member if they receive the member’s request before 6 April 2020 – see PTM164120.

12

As the pension rights will be valued on 5 April 2016 this valuation will not be affected if, after this date, the scheme reduces the member’s benefits as a result of an agreement to pay the member’s annual allowance charge (known as ‘scheme pays’ – see PTM056400).

13

Overview of valuing pre-6 April 2006 pensions in payment (Amount A)

14

Paragraph 10 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

15

The value of certain pensions in payment before 6 April 2006 (known as ‘relevant existing pensions’) uses up lifetime allowance if a member has a benefit crystallisation event (BCE) on or after 6 April 2006. However whilst relevant existing pension may use up lifetime allowance it cannot be subject to the lifetime allowance charge.

16

So a member receiving a relevant existing pension needs to include the value of this pension in the value of their pension savings as at 5 April 2016 for IP 2016.

17

How a relevant existing pension is valued depends on the form of the pension and whether or not the member has had a BCE since 6 April 2006. Where a member has more than one pension in payment ‘Amount A’ is the total of the values of each pension.

18

What is a relevant existing pension

19

Broadly, a relevant existing pension is a pension or annuity which was in payment on 5 April 2006 and derived from UK tax-relieved pension savings. Specifically, a relevant existing pension is any of the following types of pension that was in payment on 5 April 2006 (unless it is being paid to the individual as a dependant following the death of a scheme member):

20

a pension under an approved Chapter I Part 14 Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 (ICTA) retirement benefits scheme

21

a pension from a scheme formerly approved under Section 208 ICTA 1970

22

a pension under a relevant statutory scheme (defined in section 611A ICTA 1988) or a scheme treated by HMRC as if it were a relevant statutory scheme

23

an annuity (or income drawdown) under any contract relating to (a) to (c) inclusive (such annuities/pensions include bought-out benefits where the contract is in the member’s name)

24

a pension under the Parliamentary pension schemes or funds

25

an annuity from a retirement annuity contract

26

an annuity from personal pension scheme funds approved under Chapter 4 Part 14 ICTA 1988

27

income withdrawals under a personal pension scheme.

28

Amount A - valuing a pension or annuity where there has been no BCE

29

Paragraph 10 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

30

Subject to the special rule for drawdown pensions set out in the next section, the pension or annuity is valued by multiplying by 25 the annual rate at which it was payable on 5 April 2016.

31

Note that it is the annual rate that counts and not the actual amount of pension that was paid in the previous 12 months. So if, on 5 April 2016, the annual rate at which the pension or annuity is payable is £10,000 then its value for IP 2016 is £250,000 (£10,000 x 25).

32

If the member had more than one pension in payment on 5 April 2006, Amount A includes the total of the values of each pension calculated as above.

33

The value of the lump sum does not have to be taken into account as allowance is made for it in the multiplier used to value the pension.

38

income drawdown if being paid from a retirement benefits scheme or a deferred annuity contract (section 32 policy), or

39

income withdrawal if being paid from a personal pension scheme.

40

Where a member has more than one drawdown pension in payment on 5 April 2006, Amount A includes the total of the values of each drawdown pension calculated below.

41

The value of a drawdown pension is found by the formula:

42

The value of ARP will vary depending on the exact form of drawdown pension on 5 April 2016.

43

Capped drawdown

44

If on 5 April 2016 the drawdown pension is in capped drawdown (see PTM062520) ARP is the maximum amount of capped drawdown pension the member was able to take for the drawdown year which includes 6 April 2016.

45

Flexi-access drawdown previously flexible drawdown

46

If on 5 April 2016 the drawdown pension was in flexi-access drawdown but the pension had previously been in flexible drawdown (see PTM062580) ARP is the maximum amount of capped drawdown pension that would have been payable in the drawdown pension year in which the flexible drawdown declaration was accepted by the scheme administrator, had that declaration not been made.

47

Flexi-access drawdown previously capped drawdown

48

Where the member converted their capped drawdown fund to flexi-access drawdown after 5 April 2015 but before 6 April 2016 (see PTM062700) ARP is maximum annual capped drawdown pension that could have been paid in the drawdown pension year in which the conversion to flexi-access drawdown occurred, had that conversion not occurred.

49

Note that in all cases, the maximum amount is not the amount the member is actually taking each year. So, for avoidance of doubt, a drawdown pension fund doesn’t need to be reviewed to facilitate the valuation.

62

Note it is the annual rate and not the amount of pension or annuity paid in the previous 12 months.

63

For IP 2016 purposes this same value is used as the starting point, but it is then revalued using the formula

64

(25 x ARP) x (£1,250,000 / SLT)

65

68

Amount A - valuing a pre-6 April 2006 drawdown pension where there has been a BCE

78

Paragraph 10(1)(a), 10(2) to 10(5) Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

79

Paragraph 20(4) Schedule 36 Finance Act 2004

80

For IP 2016 purposes drawdown pensions that started before 6 April 2006 are valued using the formula,

81

(25 x ARP) x (£1,250,000 / SLT)

82

83

84

BCE before 6 April 2011

85

For all forms of drawdown pension ARP is the maximum annual amount that could have been paid as either unsecured pension or alternatively secured pension in the pension year that contains the date of the first BCE.

86

BCE 6 April 2011 to 5 April 2015

87

Capped drawdown – ARP is the maximum amount that could have been paid in the pension year that contains the date of the first BCE.

88

89

BCE 6 April 2015 to 5 April 2016

90

Capped drawdown - ARP is 80% of the maximum amount that could have been paid in the pension year that contains the date of the first BCE.

91

Flexi-access drawdown but previously flexible drawdown (see PTM062740) - ARP is 80% of the maximum amount that could have been paid as capped drawdown in the pension year in which the flexible drawdown declaration was accepted, had immediately before the BCE that flexible drawdown declaration had not been made.

92

Flexi-access drawdown but previously capped drawdown (see PTM062750) – ARP is 80% of the maximum amount that could have been paid as capped drawdown in the drawdown pension year in which the conversion to flexi-access drawdown took place had the conversion not taken place.

Amount B - valuing pre-6 April 2016 BCEs

100

Paragraph 11 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

101

Section 216 Finance Act 2006

102

Benefits are tested against the lifetime allowance whenever a benefit crystallisation event (BCE) occurs. BCEs can occur under registered pension schemes, and in certain circumstances non-UK pension schemes (see PTM113410).

103

How the amount of lifetime allowance that was used up is calculated will depend on the type of benefits that the member has taken. Guidance on when a BCE occurs and how to calculate the value of a BCE starts at PTM088000. For BCEs under a non-UK scheme also see the guidance starting at PTM113400.

104

It will be necessary to identify the amount of lifetime allowance used up by each BCE and then revalue it to give a value as at 5 April 2016. This is done multiplying the amount of the BCE by using the formula

105

Note that for individuals with:

106

fixed protection the standard lifetime allowance is £1.8 million where the ‘normal’ standard lifetime allowance is below this amount

107

fixed protection 2014 the standard lifetime allowance is £1.5 million where the ‘normal’ standard lifetime allowance is below this amount

108

Where a BCE has occurred under a registered pension scheme, the scheme administrator should have given the member with a BCE statement (see PTM164400) showing the percentage of the lifetime allowance used up by their BCE(s). Where the BCE occurs after 6 April 2012 for a member with a fixed protection (FP 2012), the BCE statement will be based on a percentage of £1.8 million. Similarly, where the BCE occurs after 6 April 2014 for a member with fixed protection 2014 (FP 2014) the BCE statement will be based on a percentage of £1.5 million.

109

Where they have had more than one BCE, it is the total of the revalued amounts calculated as above that is Amount B.

Overview of valuing uncrystallised rights under registered pension schemes on 5 April 2016 (Amount C)

142

Paragraph 12 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

143

Section 212 Finance Act 2004

144

Pension benefits from savings in a registered pension scheme that have not yet been taken are referred to as ‘uncrystallised rights’ in the pensions tax legislation.

145

For IP 2016 purposes, these rights are valued in the same way as for other purposes of the pension tax rules. The valuation method to be used differs depending on the type of arrangement that the member’s pension savings are in.

146

The member’s savings in a registered pension scheme could be in any - or all - of the following types of arrangement:

147

other money purchase arrangements (that is money purchase arrangements that are not cash balance arrangements)

148

cash balance arrangements

149

defined benefits arrangements, and

150

hybrid arrangements.

151

See PTM023000 for more details on the types of arrangements.

152

Where the member has more than one set of uncrystallised rights Amount C is the total value of all the member’s uncrystallised rights held under registered pension schemes.

153

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under an other money arrangement

154

Section 212(5) Finance Act 2004

155

The value of uncrystallised rights on 5 April 2016 will be the aggregate on that day of the value of any cash held under the arrangement and the market value of the other assets such as property and shares held by the arrangement. Any loans or other indebtedness should be included in the calculation. For example if a scheme has a commercial property valued at £800,000 as at 5 April 2016 and outstanding mortgage against that property of £150,000. The value for IP 2016 is £650,000 (£800,000 less the £150,000 outstanding mortgage).

156

Valuing an insurance policy which is included in a member’s pension savings

157

The Association of British Insurers (ABI) and HMRC have agreed a protocol for the valuation of insurance policies (including with-profits policies) as at 5 April 2014. This is not a detailed prescriptive method of valuing policies, rather it is a broad framework. Insurance policies should be valued in accordance with this ABI protocol for the purposes of IP 2016.

158

Any questions about valuing insurance policies as at 5 April 2016 should be raised with the insurer providing the policy.

159

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under a cash balance arrangement

160

Sections 212(4) and 277 Finance Act 2004

161

The value of uncrystallised rights on 5 April 2016 is the amount that would be available for the provision of immediate benefits if the member had been entitled to receive them on that day but subject to two valuation assumptions.

162

The two valuation assumptions are that:

163

the benefit should be calculated assuming, if the member has not already done so, that they have reached the age at which no reduction would apply under the scheme’s rules to the payment of an immediate benefit, and

164

they are deemed to be in good physical and mental health on 5 April 2016.

165

Example

166

Jordan has pension savings in a cash balance arrangement. Under her pension scheme rules, Jordan is promised a pension pot equal to £25,000 for each year of service. On 5 April 2016, Jordan, who is 57, has completed exactly 20 years of service. For IP 2016 purposes, the value of Jordan’s pension pot is £500,000 regardless of how much money is actually in her pot on 5 April 2016.

167

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under a defined benefits arrangement

168

Sections 212(6) and 277 Finance Act 2004

169

The value of uncrystallised rights on 5 April 2016 will be the total of

170

20 x the member’s gross annual pension built up to date, ignoring any potential commutation of part of the pension for a tax-free lump sum (known as a pension commencement lump sum) (PCLS), and

171

If the scheme provides a separate lump sum the value of any separate lump sum built up to date. For example if the member is in a pension scheme that provides a pension of 1/80th final salary and a separate lump sum of 3/80ths final salary for each year of pensionable service.

172

The annual pension and separate lump sum to be valued are those which would be paid if they were taken on 5 April 2016 but subject to two valuation assumptions.

173

The two valuation assumptions are that:

174

the benefit should be calculated assuming, if the member has not already done so, that they have reached the age at which no reduction would apply under the scheme’s rules to the payment of an immediate benefit, and

175

they are deemed to be in good physical and mental health on 5 April 2016.

176

Example where PCLS is by commutation

177

Lee is a member of a defined benefits scheme with an accrual rate of 1/40th of pensionable earnings for each year in the scheme.

178

On 5 April 2016 Lee is aged 55, is still in pensionable service, has pensionable earnings of £90,000 and has completed exactly 20 years pensionable service. His normal retirement age under the scheme is 65. Under the scheme’s rules, when a pension is taken before age 65, the pension is reduced by 5 per cent for each year the member is below that age. So if Lee actually took his pension on 5 April 2016 it would be reduced by 50 per cent.

179

For the purposes of the IP 2016 valuation however Lee is assumed to be 60 and so his promised pension is £45,000 (20/40 x £90,000) with a value for IP 2016 of £900,000 (20 x £45,000).

180

Example where scheme provides a separate PCLS i.e. not by commutation

181

Anna is a member of a defined benefits scheme providing a pension with an accrual rate of 1/80th and a separate lump sum of 3/80ths of pensionable earnings for each year in the scheme.

182

On 5 April 2016 Anna is aged 60, is still in pensionable service, has pensionable earnings of £80,000 and has completed exactly 30 years pensionable service. Her normal retirement age under the scheme is 67. Under the scheme’s rules, when a pension is taken before age 67, the pension is reduced by 4 per cent and the lump sum by 2 percent for each year the member is below that age. So if Anna actually took her pension and lump sum on 5 April 2016 they would be reduced by 28 per cent and 14 per cent respectively.

183

For the purposes of the IP 2016 valuation Anna is assumed to be 67 and so her promised pension is £30,000 (30/80 x £80,000) and her promised lump sum is £90,000. Amount C for IP 2016 is £690,000 calculated as

184

 (20 x £30,000) + £90,000.

185

Example where accrual rate is uneven and lump sum is by commutation

186

Victor is a member of a defined benefits scheme with an accrual rate of 1/60th of pensionable earnings for each year in the scheme up to 20 years and 2/60ths of final salary for each year of service from 20 to 30 years.

187

On 5 April 2016 Victor is aged 61, is still in pensionable service, has pensionable earnings of £60,000 and has completed exactly 25 years pensionable service. His normal retirement age under the scheme is 60. There is no late retirement uplift if benefits are taken after age 60. Victor has accrued a pension worth 30/60ths because he has 20 years’ service at 1/60th and 5 years at 2/60ths of his pensionable earnings.

188

For the purposes of the IP 2016 valuation Victor’s actual age (61) is used as this is more than 60. His promised pension is £30,000 (30/60 x £60,000) with a value for IP 2016 of £600,000 (20 x £30,000).

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under a hybrid arrangement

190

Section 212(7) and if appropriate section 277 Finance Act 2004

191

Uncrystallised pension rights are valued on the basis of whichever rights have the highest or higher value.

192

For example, a member may have rights in an arrangement to take benefits from their fund on a money purchase basis, or take benefits at say 1/120th of pensionable earnings for each year of service. Their money purchase fund and their defined benefits would both be valued as at 5 April 2016 and the higher value would apply for the purposes of IP 2016.

Amount D - valuing uncrystallised rights under relieved non-UK pension schemes on 5 April 2016

194

Paragraph 13 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

195

Paragraphs 13 to 18 Schedule 34 Finance Act 2004

196

Where pension savings under a non UK scheme have benefitted from UK tax relief those pension savings may be liable to the lifetime allowance provisions. It follows that these benefits should be included in the valuation of pension savings for IP 2016.

197

Pension savings under a non-UK pension scheme should only be included in an IP 2016 valuation if:

198

the scheme is a ‘relieved non-UK pension scheme’, and

199

the member is a ‘relieved member’.

200

See PTM113410 for guidance on the meaning of these terms.

201

The lifetime allowance provisions apply only to that part of the scheme that has befitted from UK tax relief. This is known as the ‘relevant relieved amount’. At any BCE under a relieved non-UK pension scheme’ the amount crystallised by the BCE is limited to the ‘untested portion’ of the ‘relevant relieved amount’. PTM113420 explains how to calculate the ‘relevant relieved amount’ and the ‘untested portion’. But in broad terms the untested portion is that part of the member’s relevant relieved amount that has not been subject to a BCE.

202

To find amount D assume that a BCE occurs at the end of 5 April 2016 for uncrystallised rights held under a relieved non-UK pension scheme. Amount D is the untested portion for this assumed BCE. Where the member has more than one relieved non-UK pension scheme amount D is the total of the untested portions.

203

Example

204

On 6 January 2007 Pravesh became a relieved member of a relieved non-UK pension scheme. That is the only scheme of which he is a member. Pravesh crystallises £300,000 on 6 July 2012. This is Pravesh’s first BCE from the scheme.

205

At this point Pravesh has been a relieved member of the relieved non-UK pension scheme for five and a half years. Pravesh’s total pension input amounts for the previous six tax years (2006-2007 to 2011-2012) are £400,000 and his pension input amount for the period from 6 April 2012 to 6 July 2012 is £15,000. Immediately before his BCE Pravesh’s relevant relived amount is £415,000 and this is his first BCE his untested portion is also £415,000.

206

Pravesh needs to value his pension savings for IP 2016. He has had no pension input amounts under the scheme since his BCE in June 2012. At the end on 5 April 2016 Pravesh’s relevant relieved amount is £415,000 and his untested portion is £115,000 (£415,000 less the previous £300,000 BCE).

207

Amount D for Pravesh is £115,000.

Paragraphs 9(1)(a), (1)(b) and (4) and 10 to 13 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

2

To apply for individual protection 2016 (IP 2016) a member must calculate the value of their pension savings as they stood on 5 April 2016. The way in which pension savings are valued depends on what they consist of. The valuation of an individual’s pension savings is the total of four amounts (amounts A to D) as follows:

3

Amount A - any pension that the member started to receive before 6 April 2006 ,

4

Amount B - any pension that came into payment after 5 April 2006 but before 6 April 2016 (along with certain tax-free lump sums received in the same period),

5

Amount C - pension savings that the member has not yet taken from their registered pension scheme, and

6

Amount D - pension savings that the member has not yet taken from certain overseas pension schemes.

How to obtain a valuation

15

How to obtain a valuation

16

Regulation 14C The Registered Pension Schemes (Provision of Information) Regulations 2006 – SI 2006/567

17

In order to apply for IP 2016 a valuation will need to be obtained of the value of the member’s pension savings at 5 April 2016. However, HMRC will accept asset valuations already obtained to be treated as the value of the asset on 5 April 2016 where that valuation is at a date from 31 March 2016 to 4 April 2016 and there has been no material change in the assets between that date and 5 April 2016.

18

A member can ask their pension scheme provider to give them a value for their pension savings on 5 April 2016 or tell them (if they are not already aware of it) what their annual rate of pension was on that date. A scheme administrator must give this information to the member if they receive the member’s request before 6 April 2020 – see PTM164120.

19

As the pension rights will be valued on 5 April 2016 this valuation will not be affected if, after this date, the scheme reduces the member’s benefits as a result of an agreement to pay the member’s annual allowance charge (known as ‘scheme pays’ – see PTM056400).

Overview of valuing pre-6 April 2006 pensions in payment (Amount A)

21

Paragraph 10 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

22

The value of certain pensions in payment before 6 April 2006 (known as ‘relevant existing pensions’) uses up lifetime allowance if a member has a benefit crystallisation event (BCE) on or after 6 April 2006. However whilst relevant existing pension may use up lifetime allowance it cannot be subject to the lifetime allowance charge.

23

So a member receiving a relevant existing pension needs to include the value of this pension in the value of their pension savings as at 5 April 2016 for IP 2016.

24

How a relevant existing pension is valued depends on the form of the pension and whether or not the member has had a BCE since 6 April 2006. Where a member has more than one pension in payment ‘Amount A’ is the total of the values of each pension.

25

What is a relevant existing pension

26

Broadly, a relevant existing pension is a pension or annuity which was in payment on 5 April 2006 and derived from UK tax-relieved pension savings. Specifically, a relevant existing pension is any of the following types of pension that was in payment on 5 April 2006 (unless it is being paid to the individual as a dependant following the death of a scheme member):

27

a pension under an approved Chapter I Part 14 Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 (ICTA) retirement benefits scheme

28

a pension from a scheme formerly approved under Section 208 ICTA 1970

29

a pension under a relevant statutory scheme (defined in section 611A ICTA 1988) or a scheme treated by HMRC as if it were a relevant statutory scheme

30

an annuity (or income drawdown) under any contract relating to (a) to (c) inclusive (such annuities/pensions include bought-out benefits where the contract is in the member’s name)

31

a pension under the Parliamentary pension schemes or funds

32

an annuity from a retirement annuity contract

33

an annuity from personal pension scheme funds approved under Chapter 4 Part 14 ICTA 1988

34

income withdrawals under a personal pension scheme.

Amount A - valuing a pension or annuity where there has been no BCE

36

Paragraph 10 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

37

Subject to the special rule for drawdown pensions set out in the next section, the pension or annuity is valued by multiplying by 25 the annual rate at which it was payable on 5 April 2016.

38

Note that it is the annual rate that counts and not the actual amount of pension that was paid in the previous 12 months. So if, on 5 April 2016, the annual rate at which the pension or annuity is payable is £10,000 then its value for IP 2016 is £250,000 (£10,000 x 25).

39

If the member had more than one pension in payment on 5 April 2006, Amount A includes the total of the values of each pension calculated as above.

40

The value of the lump sum does not have to be taken into account as allowance is made for it in the multiplier used to value the pension.

46

48

49

The value of ARP will vary depending on the exact form of drawdown pension on 5 April 2016.

50

Capped drawdown

51

If on 5 April 2016 the drawdown pension is in capped drawdown (see PTM062520) ARP is the maximum amount of capped drawdown pension the member was able to take for the drawdown year which includes 6 April 2016.

Flexi-access drawdown previously flexible drawdown

Overview of valuing uncrystallised rights under registered pension schemes on 5 April 2016 (Amount C)

149

Paragraph 12 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

150

Section 212 Finance Act 2004

151

Pension benefits from savings in a registered pension scheme that have not yet been taken are referred to as ‘uncrystallised rights’ in the pensions tax legislation.

152

For IP 2016 purposes, these rights are valued in the same way as for other purposes of the pension tax rules. The valuation method to be used differs depending on the type of arrangement that the member’s pension savings are in.

153

The member’s savings in a registered pension scheme could be in any - or all - of the following types of arrangement:

154

other money purchase arrangements (that is money purchase arrangements that are not cash balance arrangements)

155

cash balance arrangements

156

defined benefits arrangements, and

157

hybrid arrangements.

158

See PTM023000 for more details on the types of arrangements.

159

Where the member has more than one set of uncrystallised rights Amount C is the total value of all the member’s uncrystallised rights held under registered pension schemes.

160

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under an other money arrangement

161

Section 212(5) Finance Act 2004

162

The value of uncrystallised rights on 5 April 2016 will be the aggregate on that day of the value of any cash held under the arrangement and the market value of the other assets such as property and shares held by the arrangement. Any loans or other indebtedness should be included in the calculation. For example if a scheme has a commercial property valued at £800,000 as at 5 April 2016 and outstanding mortgage against that property of £150,000. The value for IP 2016 is £650,000 (£800,000 less the £150,000 outstanding mortgage).

163

Valuing an insurance policy which is included in a member’s pension savings

164

The Association of British Insurers (ABI) and HMRC have agreed a protocol for the valuation of insurance policies (including with-profits policies) as at 5 April 2014. This is not a detailed prescriptive method of valuing policies, rather it is a broad framework. Insurance policies should be valued in accordance with this ABI protocol for the purposes of IP 2016.

165

Any questions about valuing insurance policies as at 5 April 2016 should be raised with the insurer providing the policy.

166

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under a cash balance arrangement

167

Sections 212(4) and 277 Finance Act 2004

168

The value of uncrystallised rights on 5 April 2016 is the amount that would be available for the provision of immediate benefits if the member had been entitled to receive them on that day but subject to two valuation assumptions.

169

The two valuation assumptions are that:

170

the benefit should be calculated assuming, if the member has not already done so, that they have reached the age at which no reduction would apply under the scheme’s rules to the payment of an immediate benefit, and

171

they are deemed to be in good physical and mental health on 5 April 2016.

172

Example

173

Jordan has pension savings in a cash balance arrangement. Under her pension scheme rules, Jordan is promised a pension pot equal to £25,000 for each year of service. On 5 April 2016, Jordan, who is 57, has completed exactly 20 years of service. For IP 2016 purposes, the value of Jordan’s pension pot is £500,000 regardless of how much money is actually in her pot on 5 April 2016.

174

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under a defined benefits arrangement

175

Sections 212(6) and 277 Finance Act 2004

176

The value of uncrystallised rights on 5 April 2016 will be the total of

177

20 x the member’s gross annual pension built up to date, ignoring any potential commutation of part of the pension for a tax-free lump sum (known as a pension commencement lump sum) (PCLS), and

178

If the scheme provides a separate lump sum the value of any separate lump sum built up to date. For example if the member is in a pension scheme that provides a pension of 1/80th final salary and a separate lump sum of 3/80ths final salary for each year of pensionable service.

179

The annual pension and separate lump sum to be valued are those which would be paid if they were taken on 5 April 2016 but subject to two valuation assumptions.

180

The two valuation assumptions are that:

181

the benefit should be calculated assuming, if the member has not already done so, that they have reached the age at which no reduction would apply under the scheme’s rules to the payment of an immediate benefit, and

182

they are deemed to be in good physical and mental health on 5 April 2016.

183

Example where PCLS is by commutation

184

Lee is a member of a defined benefits scheme with an accrual rate of 1/40th of pensionable earnings for each year in the scheme.

185

On 5 April 2016 Lee is aged 55, is still in pensionable service, has pensionable earnings of £90,000 and has completed exactly 20 years pensionable service. His normal retirement age under the scheme is 65. Under the scheme’s rules, when a pension is taken before age 65, the pension is reduced by 5 per cent for each year the member is below that age. So if Lee actually took his pension on 5 April 2016 it would be reduced by 50 per cent.

186

For the purposes of the IP 2016 valuation however Lee is assumed to be 60 and so his promised pension is £45,000 (20/40 x £90,000) with a value for IP 2016 of £900,000 (20 x £45,000).

187

Example where scheme provides a separate PCLS i.e. not by commutation

188

Anna is a member of a defined benefits scheme providing a pension with an accrual rate of 1/80th and a separate lump sum of 3/80ths of pensionable earnings for each year in the scheme.

189

On 5 April 2016 Anna is aged 60, is still in pensionable service, has pensionable earnings of £80,000 and has completed exactly 30 years pensionable service. Her normal retirement age under the scheme is 67. Under the scheme’s rules, when a pension is taken before age 67, the pension is reduced by 4 per cent and the lump sum by 2 percent for each year the member is below that age. So if Anna actually took her pension and lump sum on 5 April 2016 they would be reduced by 28 per cent and 14 per cent respectively.

190

For the purposes of the IP 2016 valuation Anna is assumed to be 67 and so her promised pension is £30,000 (30/80 x £80,000) and her promised lump sum is £90,000. Amount C for IP 2016 is £690,000 calculated as

191

 (20 x £30,000) + £90,000.

192

Example where accrual rate is uneven and lump sum is by commutation

193

Victor is a member of a defined benefits scheme with an accrual rate of 1/60th of pensionable earnings for each year in the scheme up to 20 years and 2/60ths of final salary for each year of service from 20 to 30 years.

194

On 5 April 2016 Victor is aged 61, is still in pensionable service, has pensionable earnings of £60,000 and has completed exactly 25 years pensionable service. His normal retirement age under the scheme is 60. There is no late retirement uplift if benefits are taken after age 60. Victor has accrued a pension worth 30/60ths because he has 20 years’ service at 1/60th and 5 years at 2/60ths of his pensionable earnings.

195

For the purposes of the IP 2016 valuation Victor’s actual age (61) is used as this is more than 60. His promised pension is £30,000 (30/60 x £60,000) with a value for IP 2016 of £600,000 (20 x £30,000).

Amount C - valuing uncrystallised rights under a hybrid arrangement

197

Section 212(7) and if appropriate section 277 Finance Act 2004

198

Uncrystallised pension rights are valued on the basis of whichever rights have the highest or higher value.

199

For example, a member may have rights in an arrangement to take benefits from their fund on a money purchase basis, or take benefits at say 1/120th of pensionable earnings for each year of service. Their money purchase fund and their defined benefits would both be valued as at 5 April 2016 and the higher value would apply for the purposes of IP 2016.

Amount D - valuing uncrystallised rights under relieved non-UK pension schemes on 5 April 2016

201

Paragraph 13 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2016

202

Paragraphs 13 to 18 Schedule 34 Finance Act 2004

203

Where pension savings under a non UK scheme have benefitted from UK tax relief those pension savings may be liable to the lifetime allowance provisions. It follows that these benefits should be included in the valuation of pension savings for IP 2016.

204

Pension savings under a non-UK pension scheme should only be included in an IP 2016 valuation if:

205

the scheme is a ‘relieved non-UK pension scheme’, and

206

the member is a ‘relieved member’.

207

See PTM113410 for guidance on the meaning of these terms.

208

The lifetime allowance provisions apply only to that part of the scheme that has befitted from UK tax relief. This is known as the ‘relevant relieved amount’. At any BCE under a relieved non-UK pension scheme’ the amount crystallised by the BCE is limited to the ‘untested portion’ of the ‘relevant relieved amount’. PTM113420 explains how to calculate the ‘relevant relieved amount’ and the ‘untested portion’. But in broad terms the untested portion is that part of the member’s relevant relieved amount that has not been subject to a BCE.

209

To find amount D assume that a BCE occurs at the end of 5 April 2016 for uncrystallised rights held under a relieved non-UK pension scheme. Amount D is the untested portion for this assumed BCE. Where the member has more than one relieved non-UK pension scheme amount D is the total of the untested portions.

210

Example

211
